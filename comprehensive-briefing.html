Executive Summary

The ROE Conversion Gap

BMO delivered adjusted revenue growth of approximately 12% in fiscal 2025 and reported record revenue across all segments in Q1 2026. Yet its return on equity (ROE) of 11.3% (FY2025 adjusted) still trails RBC by ~550 basis points. The gap is not about top-line generation—it’s about the cost and composition of the workforce deployed to generate that revenue.

Fermi Estimation Methodology (3 Methods)

Method 1 — Corporate Segment NIE Proxy: Each bank’s “Corporate/Other” NIE ÷ Total NIE, normalized for allocation differences (BMO retains most T&O costs in Corporate Services; peers allocate to LOBs).

Method 2 — Technology Spending Anchor: Where tech spend is known, estimate tech headcount (Spend × 65% labor share ÷ avg compensation), then gross up ×1.4 for full TOCA.

Method 3 — Industry Benchmarks: Large universal banks: 28–38% support staff (McKinsey, Accenture, Oliver Wyman). Positioned each bank by complexity, M&A status, tech intensity.

All estimates carry ±3–5 pp uncertainty.

Headcount Dynamics

Bank of the West: The Integration Tail

BMO’s $16.3B acquisition of Bank of the West (Feb 2023) was transformational—doubling the U.S. footprint and adding ~9,300 employees. While the technology conversion won Celent’s 2024 Model Bank award, the workforce integration has been a longer tail.

BMO Headcount Trajectory (FY2022–FY2025)

Year-over-Year Headcount Change (%)

Integration Staffing Overhead Back-office, operations, compliance, and technology integration required maintaining duplicate staffing through much of 2023–2024. Headcount reductions of ~4.3% in FY2024 and ~0.7% in FY2025 are largely normalization of post-acquisition bloat.
U.S. Loan Balances Declining In Q4 FY2025, U.S. loan balances fell 2% and deposits were down 5% as BMO pruned its U.S. portfolio—exiting RV loans, indirect auto, franchise lending, and lower-return credit cards. The workforce supports a shrinking asset base.
Branch Divestitures Underway BMO announced the sale of 138 branches in select U.S. markets, with a $102M goodwill write-down in Q4 2025. Geographic rationalization of the physical footprint—and associated staff—is still in progress.
Revenue Hire Ramp-Up BMO has hired 100+ commercial bankers and private advisors in the past 12 months. These hires need 12–18 months to build relationships; meaningful loan growth expected by Q2/Q3 2026. This signals recognition of underinvestment in front-line revenue capacity.
Hypothesis: Revenue-Producing Role Ratio is Temporarily Skewed Using internal workforce data, analyze the ratio of “Revenue-Producing Roles” (relationship managers, commercial bankers, wealth advisors, capital markets professionals) to “Revenue-Support Roles” (operations, compliance, technology, HR, finance) over FY2022–FY2025. The hypothesis is that BMO’s ratio has been temporarily skewed toward support roles due to integration demands.

US Workforce Share vs Estimated US Earnings Share

US Exposure vs Total Turnover Rate

If approximately 30–35% of BMO’s business is in the US, and US operations require 1.3–1.5× the compliance and support infrastructure per revenue dollar compared to Canadian operations, that alone could explain a significant portion of the TOCA gap versus peers like RBC, CIBC, and National Bank who have minimal US retail banking exposure.

Regulatory Overhead US regulatory compliance costs (Dodd-Frank, CFPB, OCC, FDIC, state-level, BSA/AML) are significantly more prescriptive than OSFI/Canadian regulation. Foreign banks with >$100B in US assets must maintain a US Intermediate Holding Company (IHC) with its own capital, liquidity, and stress testing requirements.
Higher US Voluntary Turnover BMO data shows US voluntary turnover at 12.2% vs 9.8% in Canada—a 24% premium. TD shows a similar pattern: 11.4% US vs 10.1% Canada. Higher turnover drives recurring recruiting costs, training investment, productivity loss, and knowledge attrition.
US Compensation Premiums US banking salaries run materially higher than Canadian equivalents, especially in technology and compliance. The Bank of the West footprint (California, Colorado) carries some of the highest labor costs in North America.
Duplicate Infrastructure US operations require separate risk management, compliance, legal, technology, and operational functions. This creates a structural floor on non-revenue-producing headcount that purely domestic banks do not carry.
Enhanced Prudential Standards (EPS) — The Hidden Multiplier Foreign Banking Organizations (FBOs) with >$100B in US total consolidated assets must establish a US Intermediate Holding Company (IHC) subject to separate US capital requirements, US liquidity requirements (LCR), US stress testing (CCAR/DFAST), US resolution planning, and US risk management standards—all separate from and in addition to Canadian OSFI requirements. This is effectively a mandate to build a second bank-within-a-bank, with its own governance, risk, and compliance apparatus. Both BMO and TD exceed this threshold.

TD’s $4.2B AML enforcement penalty in 2024 illustrates the extreme tail risk of US operations when compliance infrastructure proves inadequate. This event alone consumed approximately 47% of TD’s FY2024 reported net income and triggered a 10% US asset cap—a regulatory constraint with no equivalent in the Canadian framework.

Quantifying the US Cost-to-Serve Effect

What this means: A portion of BMO’s elevated Corporate Services NIE (32.6% of total, vs peer range of 16–25%) is likely structural—driven by the regulatory and operational requirements of running a large US retail bank—rather than purely attributable to organizational inefficiency.

Estimating the effect: If US operations require ~1.4× the support cost per revenue dollar (conservative given duplicate infrastructure, higher turnover, and EPS compliance), and BMO derives ~33% of revenue from the US, that implies ~4.6 percentage points of incremental TOCA burden (33% × 0.4 × ~35% base TOCA rate). This alone narrows the gap between BMO and domestically-focused peers significantly.

What remains unexplained: Even adjusting for US structural costs, BMO’s overhead appears elevated. The Bank of the West integration contributed transitional overhead that should normalize. The remaining gap may reflect genuine optimization opportunity—but the size of that opportunity is smaller than the raw TOCA comparison suggests.

Synthesis

Ranked Workforce Hypotheses

The primary workforce hypotheses, in order of likely impact, based on available evidence:

  1. Post-Acquisition Workforce Normalization Lag — Bank of the West integration creating temporary support-role bloat and ongoing severance charges. The headcount reductions of ~4.3% in FY2024 and ~0.7% in FY2025 are normalization, but the process is incomplete.
  2. U.S. Banking Workforce Productivity Deficit — Shrinking U.S. loan book + divesting branches + new banker ramp = temporary revenue/employee depression. U.S. Banking ROE improved 170 bps to 8.1% in FY2025 but remains well below corporate targets.
  3. Incomplete Digital-to-Headcount Conversion — Strong digital investment (~$1.3B over 2023–2024) not yet fully reflected in non-revenue role displacement or revenue redeployment. AI tools deployed but efficiency gains depend on adoption rates.
  4. Dual-Jurisdiction Regulatory Staffing Overhead — OSFI + OCC/FDIC compliance requirements consuming disproportionate non-revenue headcount. Enhanced Prudential Standards mandate a “bank-within-a-bank” for the US operations.
  5. Geographic Cost Structure Inherited from Bank of the West — California/Colorado premium labor costs diluting U.S. segment efficiency. Bay Area layoffs and 138-branch divestiture signal ongoing geographic cost optimization.

Additional Workforce Factors for Investigation

Turnover & Retention: Industry-wide attrition in operational and technology roles has been elevated due to competition from fintechs, tech companies, and consulting firms. High turnover creates productivity drag and recurring recruitment/training costs. The BMORE program (skills-based hiring in Chicago/Milwaukee) noted retention challenges, investing in mentorship to improve outcomes.

Workforce Aging & Tenure: Bank of the West brought a legacy workforce with different tenure profiles. Longer-tenured employees in non-revenue roles may have higher costs but lower adaptability to digital transformation. Conversely, experienced relationship managers who departed during integration uncertainty represent direct revenue capacity loss.

Time-in-Position & Internal Mobility: Post-acquisition integration often creates organizational paralysis—employees frozen in current roles awaiting org structure clarity. This depresses internal mobility rates, a leading indicator of engagement and the ability to redeploy talent from support to revenue roles.

Digital Adoption & AI Readiness: BMO’s AI tools (Lumi, Rover, BMO Assist) are deployed, but efficiency gains depend on employee adoption rates. Legacy systems in the U.S. segment and different digital maturity among former BoW staff can limit real-world impact.

Location-Based Cost & Productivity: The BoW footprint (California, Colorado, western U.S.) carries premium labor costs. San Francisco Bay Area staff command premium compensation. BMO’s post-acquisition layoffs (228 employees in Bay Area per WARN filings) and branch divestiture suggest ongoing geographic cost optimization.

Regulatory & Compliance Staffing Burden: Operating under both OSFI (Canada) and OCC (U.S.) imposes dual compliance staffing. FDIC special assessments, OSFI B-15 climate risk guidelines, and E-21 operational resilience requirements all demand non-revenue headcount.
