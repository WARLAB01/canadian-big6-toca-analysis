Context

Why Turnover & US Exposure Matter

The original TOCA analysis found BMO carries the highest estimated non-revenue-producing headcount ratio among the Big 6. This companion analysis tests two explanatory variables: employee turnover patterns (do banks with higher turnover carry more support overhead?) and US revenue concentration (does operating in the more heavily regulated US market require disproportionate support infrastructure?).

Key Finding: US Operations Drive Higher Turnover Where geographic data is available (BMO, TD), US voluntary turnover runs 12-25% higher than Canadian voluntary turnover. Methodology & Data Gaps

Sources: All turnover data extracted from FY2024 sustainability reports published by each bank between March-April 2025. KPMG-verified where indicated (BNS, RBC).

Definition Differences: Banks define "turnover" slightly differently. BMO and TD provide explicit voluntary/involuntary splits by geography. RBC provides splits but groups US with UK and other international geographies. CIBC provides voluntary only at the global level. BNS provides voluntary and total only (involuntary calculated). NA does not disclose turnover rates.

Revenue-Generating Turnover: No bank publicly discloses turnover specifically for revenue-generating (LOB) vs support (TOCA) roles. This remains a data gap. The geographic split (Canada vs US) serves as the best available proxy, since US operations are predominantly revenue-generating retail and commercial banking.

Exclusions: RBC figures exclude City National Bank and RBC Brewin Dolphin (not yet on primary HR platform). Scotiabank excludes certain subsidiaries. CIBC excludes CIBC Caribbean, temporary employees, and contingent workers.

Correlation: US Exposure & Turnover The three banks with the highest total turnover rates (BNS 17.5%, BMO 16.6%, TD 15.0%) all have significant international or US operations. Section 3

The US Cost-to-Serve Thesis

This is potentially a stronger explanatory variable for BMO's higher TOCA ratio than pure organizational bloat.

If approximately 30-35% of BMO's business is in the US, and US operations require 1.3-1.5x the compliance and support infrastructure per revenue dollar compared to Canadian operations, that alone could explain a significant portion of the TOCA gap versus peers like RBC, CIBC, and National Bank who have minimal US retail banking exposure.

BMO and TD — the two banks with the largest US footprints — face a compounding set of structural cost drivers that do not apply (or apply far less) to their domestically-focused peers:

Regulatory Overhead US regulatory compliance costs (Dodd-Frank, CFPB, OCC, FDIC, state-level regulations, BSA/AML) are significantly more prescriptive than OSFI/Canadian regulation. Foreign banks with >$100B in US assets (BMO and TD both qualify) must maintain a US Intermediate Holding Company (IHC) with its own capital, liquidity, and stress testing requirements.
Higher US Voluntary Turnover BMO data shows US voluntary turnover at 12.2% vs 9.8% in Canada — a 24% premium. TD shows a similar pattern: 11.4% US vs 10.1% Canada. Higher turnover drives recruiting costs, training investment, productivity loss during ramp-up periods, and knowledge attrition.
US Compensation Premiums US banking salaries run materially higher than Canadian equivalents, especially in technology and compliance roles. This compounds the cost of maintaining the duplicate infrastructure that US regulations require.
Duplicate Infrastructure US operations require separate risk management, compliance, legal, technology, and operational functions to meet US regulatory standards. This creates a structural floor on non-revenue-producing headcount that purely domestic banks do not carry.
Enhanced Prudential Standards (EPS) — The Hidden Multiplier Foreign Banking Organizations (FBOs) with >$100B in US total consolidated assets are subject to Enhanced Prudential Standards under the Dodd-Frank Act. Both BMO and TD exceed this threshold. This requires them to establish a US Intermediate Holding Company (IHC) subject to US capital requirements, US liquidity requirements (LCR), US stress testing (CCAR/DFAST), US resolution planning, and US risk management standards — all separate from and in addition to their Canadian OSFI requirements. This is effectively a mandate to build a second bank-within-a-bank, with its own governance, risk, and compliance apparatus.

TD's $4.2B AML enforcement penalty in 2024 illustrates the extreme tail risk of US operations when compliance infrastructure proves inadequate. This event alone consumed approximately 47% of TD's FY2024 reported net income ($8.8B) and triggered a 10% US asset cap — a regulatory constraint with no equivalent in the Canadian framework.

Implications for the TOCA Analysis

What this means: A portion of BMO's elevated Corporate Services NIE (32.6% of total, vs peer range of 16-25%) is likely structural — driven by the regulatory and operational requirements of running a large US retail bank — rather than purely attributable to organizational inefficiency.

Quantifying the effect: If US operations require ~1.4x the support cost per revenue dollar (a conservative estimate given duplicate infrastructure, higher turnover, and EPS compliance), and BMO derives ~33% of revenue from the US, that implies ~4.6 percentage points of incremental TOCA burden (33% × 0.4 × ~35% base TOCA rate). This alone would narrow the gap between BMO and domestically-focused peers significantly.

What remains unexplained: Even adjusting for US structural costs, BMO's overhead appears elevated. The Bank of the West integration (completed 2023) likely contributed transitional overhead that should normalize. The remaining gap may reflect genuine optimization opportunity in technology and operations — but the size of that opportunity is smaller than the raw TOCA comparison suggests.
